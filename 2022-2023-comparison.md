# 2022 to 2023 Comparison

Sources:
- `2022StateoftheStateBook.pdf`
- `2023SOTSBook.pdf`
- `2022-first-pass-inventory.csv`
- `2023-first-pass-inventory.csv`

## Bottom line

The 2023 agenda does not look like a reset. It looks more like a narrowing and intensification of the 2022 agenda.

2022 is broader and more omnibus. It covers nearly every major governing domain in a single first-year agenda.
2023 is more concentrated and feels like a second-year governing program that doubles down on a smaller set of priorities:

- housing
- mental health
- public safety
- health care system reform
- climate and affordability
- education
- business growth and semiconductors

So the answer to the continuity question is: there is substantial overlap at the level that matters for accountability, even where the exact proposal titles differ.

## What clearly carries forward from 2022 into 2023

### 1. Housing remains a top-tier governing priority

2022 already included major housing production, supportive housing, transit-oriented development, office/hotel conversion, safe accessory dwelling units, and homelessness initiatives.

2023 turns housing into the first and most prominent section of the book through the New York Housing Compact. It pushes harder on:
- production goals
- local approval pressure
- transit-oriented housing
- office conversion
- basement apartments
- tenant protection
- lead remediation

Interpretation:
- 2023 is not a new housing priority.
- It is a more explicit and more forceful version of the 2022 housing agenda.

### 2. Mental health becomes a standalone flagship issue

In 2022, mental health sits inside the broader health and human services agenda.
In 2023, it becomes its own major section.

The continuation is obvious:
- insurance coverage expansion
- youth mental health
- psychiatric capacity
- supportive housing for people with mental illness
- care coordination and accountability

Interpretation:
- this is a classic example of a theme moving from one component of a broader agenda into a signature administration priority.

### 3. Public safety and violence reduction continue, with more emphasis on system capacity

2022 emphasized:
- gun tracing
- crime analysis centers
- law enforcement support
- community-based gun violence response
- pretrial services

2023 continues the same lane, but pushes more into:
- alternatives to incarceration
- reentry
- State Police expansion
- prosecutors
- discovery and bail
- parole supervision
- fentanyl
- cyber and fire-service capacity

Interpretation:
- same overall governing bucket, but 2023 broadens from gun violence and enforcement support into a fuller criminal justice and public safety systems agenda.

### 4. Health care remains a major structural priority

2022 health care is heavily workforce- and equity-oriented.
2023 health care looks more like delivery-system reform:
- capital funding
- technology capital
- staffing practices
- project approvals
- Medicaid and Essential Plan coverage
- primary care
- EMS
- long-term care
- public health readiness

Interpretation:
- this is continuity with evolution.
- 2022 focuses on rebuilding after pandemic strain; 2023 focuses on system redesign and capacity.

### 5. Climate, energy, and affordability continue

2022 centered:
- offshore wind
- energy storage
- electrification
- school buses
- EVs
- environmental bond act
- recycling and water resources

2023 continues the same family of issues through:
- cap-and-invest
- affordability
- buildings
- transportation electrification
- just transition
- waste and recycling
- water quality
- PFAS

Interpretation:
- the administration is still in the same climate-energy-environment lane, but 2023 frames it more around implementation and affordability.

### 6. Education and workforce pathways continue

2022 included:
- teacher pipeline
- foundation aid
- childcare
- tutoring / school climate-adjacent supports
- degree attainment
- SUNY/CUNY redesign

2023 continues with:
- public education investment
- pre-K
- tutoring
- early college / P-TECH
- high school to college to workforce pathways
- SUNY/CUNY admissions and funding

Interpretation:
- again, a continuation rather than a reset.

## What looks more new or more elevated in 2023

These themes exist in weaker or less central form in 2022 but appear more prominently in 2023:

- the Housing Compact as a statewide production framework
- mental health as a standalone flagship agenda
- semiconductor and technology-sector economic development
- explicit affordability pressure through minimum wage indexing
- stronger criminal-justice-system operating issues like discovery, bail, prosecutors, and parole

## Agency continuity

At a high level, the same agency clusters continue to dominate across both years:

- Homes and Community Renewal
- DOH / OMH and related health agencies
- DCJS / State Police / DOCCS
- NYSERDA / DEC
- State Education Department / SUNY / CUNY
- Empire State Development
- DOT and transportation actors
- Agriculture & Markets

This is important for your eventual outreach strategy:
- the administration’s agenda appears to be concentrated in a recurring set of agencies, not scattered randomly across all of government.

## Practical implication for the project

The comparison suggests the full five-year exercise should not treat each year as a totally separate universe.

Instead, the better model is:
- identify each year’s discrete commitments
- then tag whether each commitment is:
  - new
  - a continuation of a prior-year goal
  - an expansion or quantification of a prior-year goal
  - a status/implementation follow-on

That will let the final product answer two important questions:
- what did the Governor promise in a given year?
- which priorities persisted and deepened across years?

## Current caution

The 2022 first-pass inventory is structurally solid.
The 2023 first-pass inventory is good enough for high-level thematic comparison, but it still needs cleanup before a strict row-to-row overlap count would be reliable.
